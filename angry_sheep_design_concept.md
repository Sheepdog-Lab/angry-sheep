# Angry Sheep — Design Concept Reference

## Project Overview

**Project Name:** Angry Sheep
**Setting:** Children's Museum (Pittsburgh)
**Target Audience:** Children ages 6–8
**Core Goal:** Build emotional resilience through collaborative problem-solving
**Format:** Physical + digital hybrid interactive exhibit (tabletop projection with tangible objects)
**Studio Context:** MD MPS Studio course at CMU

---

## Research Foundation

### Participatory Design Research

The design concept is grounded in participatory design research conducted with children and families. The research approach shifted from testing pre-designed challenges to generative participatory design — having kids create solutions rather than test pre-built concepts.

- **Methodology:** Scenario-based activities using figurines in a sandbox setting. A parent receives a card with a frustration scenario to act out, and the child uses figurines to demonstrate support methods. This avoids the awkwardness of direct parent-child role play.
- **Support method categories explored:** Body-based comfort (hugs, physical presence), verbal support, and other creative approaches.
- **Key research focus:** Emotional support rather than problem-solving mechanics. The goal is to help children build emotional resilience through social support, going beyond just "encouragement" to broader support methods.

### Key Workshop Findings

- Kids are drawn to physical materials and prefer **doing over being told**.
- They need **immediate visual feedback** to understand what works.
- Kids are comfortable with **task support** (helping someone do something) but **struggle with emotional support** — this is the key design opportunity the exhibit targets.

## Design Concept: Collaborative Sheep Herding

### How It Works

A **translucent circular table** displays a **projected scene** with digital sheep wandering around. The projector and camera are both mounted *beneath* the tabletop — the projector throws the scene up through the glass, while the camera reads ArUco fiducial markers printed on the undersides of each physical tool. A **built-in microphone and speaker** are also embedded in the table itself — the mic picks up children's voices for keyword-based emotional mechanics, and the speaker plays sheep sounds, calming audio, and celebration music. An open-sided pen sits at the center of the scene. Children work together using **physical tools placed on the table** to guide the sheep into the pen.

### Physical Tools

1. **Blocks** — Create barriers and channels to funnel sheep toward the pen.
2. **Sheepdogs** — Actively steer and push sheep in a direction.
3. **Grass objects** — Attract and feed sheep (used for calming and luring).
4. **Brushes** — Physical grooming tools children use to brush and pet the digital sheep's fur; a core calming action during crisis mode.

### Emotional Mechanics (Crisis Mode)

This is the core of the experience:

1. **Trigger:** When a sheep is pushed roughly 3 times in a row (after the child understands the basic goal), it enters crisis mode.
2. **Crisis behavior:** The sheep turns red, moves erratically, speeds up, and stops responding to the sheepdogs. Rough handling can also cause sheep to multiply, making the task harder.
3. **De-escalation methods (the only way to succeed):**
   - Feeding the sheep grass
   - **Brushing/petting** the projected sheep with the physical brush tool on the table surface
   - Speaking encouragement into the **built-in table microphone** (keyword detection recognizes supportive language; triggers a calming response on nearby angry sheep)
4. **Hint system:** If a child is unsuccessful for 1 minute, a hint bubble appears suggesting a calming action (e.g., a feeding icon or petting animation).
5. **Timer:** A 2–4 minute timer creates urgency without being overwhelming.

### Celebration & Feedback

- **Small wins:** Celebration animation each time a single sheep is successfully guided into the pen.
- **Big celebration:** A larger reward animation when all sheep are corralled.
- Visual feedback clearly shows sheep emotional states throughout (color changes, movement speed).
- **Audio feedback** through the built-in table speaker reinforces emotional states — ambient sheep sounds, agitated bleats during crisis, calming tones as sheep de-escalate, and celebration music on wins.

## Core Design Principles

1. **Shared agency** — Individual exploration that naturally invites collaboration. Multiple children can engage around the table simultaneously with different tools.
2. **Emotional regulation through system response** — The game *shows* children what works through the sheep's reactions, rather than *telling* them what to do. No direct instruction.
3. **Immediate visual feedback** — Children see the results of their actions right away so they can learn cause and effect.
4. **Multiple entry points** — Different tools and roles allow children to join in at any time without needing onboarding from another player.

## Alternative Concept Explored (Not Selected)

**Maze Navigation System:** Two-player cooperative game — Player A controls a character in a first-person maze via joystick, Player B stands on an elevated platform with a full maze overview and gives directions. This concept was set aside due to concerns about one player becoming too bossy/directive and the other feeling frustrated or disengaged.

## Instructor & Reviewer Feedback

### Positive Reception
- The tangible/digital mix and narrative approach were praised.
- The sheep herding metaphor provides a clear, engaging way to practice collaboration and emotional support skills.
- Farm theme resonates well with a Pennsylvania audience.

### Recommendations

**Materials & Physical Design:**
- Use **multi-sensory textures** — e.g., astroturf for grass, varied block materials.
- Make objects **larger scale** to prevent theft/loss in a museum environment.
- Use **museum-quality, unique materials** rather than generic plastic toys (children treat unique objects with more care).

**Language & Emotional Development:**
- The microphone can be used to **prompt empathy vocabulary** — guiding children toward supportive language.
- Reference **Fred Rogers Productions / Daniel Tiger strategies** for age-appropriate emotional vocabulary.
- Consider a **take-home component** so families can continue practicing emotional support skills at home.

**Accessibility:**
- The concept has strong **universal design potential**, particularly for children with autism or sensory needs.

**Presentation & Communication:**
- Use **animations/icons over text instructions** for the target age group.
- Design clear **affordances** for each physical element so children intuitively understand what each tool does.
